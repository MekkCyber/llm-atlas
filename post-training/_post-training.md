# Post-Training

*Taxonomy — the techniques that turn a pretrained base model into an aligned, useful assistant.*

**TL;DR:** A base model predicts next tokens; it does not follow instructions, refuse harmful requests, or reason step-by-step on demand. Post-training is the collection of techniques that *shape* behavior without retraining from scratch. Modern pipelines stack several: **SFT** (supervised fine-tune on curated instructions) → **preference optimization** (DPO or PPO-with-RM) → **[RLVR](rlvr.md)** (RL with programmatic rewards, for verifiable capabilities like math and code). Each stage fixes a different failure mode; skipping one leaves a specific gap.

**Related taxonomies:** [_rl](_rl.md) — the RL half in depth (policy gradient, PPO, GRPO, KL). [_rewards](_rewards.md) — the reward-signal half (rule-based, ORM, PRM, preference RM, shaping).
**Depth files covered here:** [rlvr](rlvr.md) · [grpo](grpo.md) · [rejection-sampling](rejection-sampling.md) · [long-cot-rl](reasoning/long-cot-rl.md) · [orm](reasoning/orm.md) · [prm](reasoning/prm.md) · [mcts](reasoning/mcts.md)

## The problem

A pretrained base model is a next-token predictor on web text. Asked "What's the capital of France?" it might produce "What's the capital of Germany?" (continuing a quiz format) or "Paris. Located at..." (continuing an encyclopedia article) or "is a common question on trivia sites" (continuing a meta-commentary). Useful, aligned behavior — answer the question concisely, refuse harmful requests, reason through complex problems — is not in its training distribution at inference time.

Post-training is the collective name for techniques that modify a pretrained model's behavior without redoing pretraining. The axes it has to shape:

- **Format** — follow instruction-response conventions, use chat templates.
- **Helpfulness / style** — calibrated tone, useful length, appropriate hedging.
- **Safety** — refuse harmful requests, avoid PII, follow content policies.
- **Capability on verifiable tasks** — math, code, structured output.
- **Reasoning** — step-by-step thinking on hard problems.

No single technique covers all of these. The modern recipe stacks multiple, each targeting its own axis.

## The shared pattern

Every post-training technique modifies weights in response to one of three signal types:

| Signal type | What's provided per example | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Supervised** | A reference completion to imitate | SFT, behavior cloning |
| **Preference** | A ranking between two (or more) completions | DPO, RLHF, KTO, IPO |
| **Reward** | A scalar on each completion | PPO with RM, [RLVR](rlvr.md), GRPO |

The pipeline shape is: coarse signals first (supervised teach format), finer signals last (preference and reward sharpen specific behaviors). In between, the model weights move from "generic next-token predictor" to "aligned assistant" by stages.

## Variants

### Supervised

| Technique | What it does | Main tradeoff | When it wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **SFT** (instruction tuning) | Next-token loss on curated (prompt, response) pairs | Needs high-quality labeled data; can't easily get better than the labels | Always the first post-training step; teaches format and broad helpfulness |
| Distillation SFT | SFT on completions generated by a stronger teacher model | Free labels, limited by teacher's ceiling | Scaling SFT data when human labels are scarce; R1-trace → Qwen/Llama is the canonical recipe |
| [Rejection-sampling SFT](rejection-sampling.md) | Generate K candidates, score each, SFT on the best | Compute-heavy per label; requires a scorer | Self-improvement loops; Llama 3 uses heavily; R1 Stage 3 (~800k samples) |

### Preference optimization

| Technique | What it does | Main tradeoff | When it wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **DPO** | Directly optimizes log-ratio of preferred vs. rejected completions, no RM | No reward model to train/serve; simpler than PPO-RLHF | Modern default for preference tuning; Tülu 3, Llama 3 |
| PPO with learned RM (classical RLHF) | Train reward model on preferences, then PPO against it | Reward model can be hacked; more infra | Historical (InstructGPT); rare in new open pipelines |
| RLAIF / Constitutional AI | AI-generated preference labels | Scales label generation; relies on judge quality | When human preferences are expensive |
| KTO (Kahneman-Tversky Optimization) | Prospect-theory-inspired variant of DPO, uses single samples not pairs | No need for paired data | When you only have binary good/bad labels, not pairs |
| IPO (Identity Preference Optimization) | DPO variant more robust to preference-label noise | Slightly different loss, less studied | Research; mitigates DPO over-fitting to noisy labels |

### Reward-based

| Technique | What it does | Main tradeoff | When it wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **[RLVR](rlvr.md)** | PPO/GRPO with programmatic verifier as reward (math answer match, test pass, format check) | Only applicable to verifiable domains | **Reasoning and code capability** — Tülu 3, DeepSeek-R1, Kimi K1.5 |
| [GRPO](grpo.md) | Group-relative PPO variant, no value model needed | Requires K rollouts per prompt | Modern default policy optimizer for RLVR |
| PPO with learned RM | See above under preference | See above | See above |
| [Process reward models (PRM)](reasoning/prm.md) | RM scores *steps* of reasoning, not just final answer | Needs step-level labels; prone to reward hacking | Reranking at inference; R1 tried and rejected for RL |
| [Outcome reward models (ORM)](reasoning/orm.md) | Learned verifier trained on final-answer labels | Same hackability as any learned RM | Reranking; RL reward where no rule verifier applies |
| [Long-CoT RL](reasoning/long-cot-rl.md) | RLVR from base, no SFT — grows long reasoning traces | Only works with a strong base; needs many RL steps | Eliciting reasoning without CoT demos — DeepSeek-R1-Zero |

## How to choose

**For a modern open-source instruct model, the [Tülu 3](../case-studies/olmo-2.md) recipe is the current canonical pipeline:**

```
Base model
    ↓
SFT on curated instruction mixes
    ↓
DPO with high-quality preference data
    ↓
RLVR (GRPO) on verifiable-reward tasks (math, code, format)
    ↓
Instruct model
```

Each stage has a distinct role:

- **SFT** teaches the format and broad helpful behavior. The instruct model "looks right" only after SFT.
- **DPO** shapes style, refusal behavior, and non-verifiable quality. Closes the "helpful-but-not-quite-aligned" gap after SFT.
- **[RLVR](rlvr.md)** pushes the model to actually *solve* verifiable problems — math, code, structured outputs. This is where the reasoning benchmark numbers come from.

Skipping stages has predictable consequences:

- Skip SFT → DPO/RLVR don't converge because the base model is nowhere near the right distribution.
- Skip DPO → model is competent on verifiable tasks but has rough style, refusal, and helpfulness.
- Skip RLVR → model is well-behaved but weak on math/code benchmarks relative to peers.

**For closed-frontier labs, the pattern is similar but with substantial proprietary additions:** iterative SFT + preference rounds, RM-based PPO at scale (still common at Anthropic and OpenAI), safety-specific red-teaming loops, constitutional AI or similar principles-based training, extensive rejection sampling. The open-source stack is a simplification of this frontier stack, not a different architecture.

**PPO with a learned reward model** is increasingly rare in new open pipelines because DPO is cheaper and [RLVR](rlvr.md) is more robust. It's still used when the task genuinely needs a learned scorer (helpfulness, tone) at high training volumes.

## Adjacent but distinct

- **Parameter-efficient fine-tuning** (LoRA, QLoRA, adapters). Same signal types as above, but updates only a small slice of parameters. Orthogonal to *which* post-training technique you pick. See [fine-tuning/](fine-tuning/).
- **Continued pretraining** (domain adaptation with next-token loss). Sits between pretraining and post-training; not covered here.
- **Tool-use / agent training**. Uses [RLVR](rlvr.md)-style verifiable rewards for tool-call correctness; overlap with this taxonomy but typically discussed as its own topic.
- **Evaluation of post-trained models**. How you measure success (preference evals, capability benchmarks, refusal tests) is its own space.
- **Inference-time search** (MCTS, best-of-N). Discussed in [reasoning/mcts.md](reasoning/mcts.md) but orthogonal to training — a test-time compute lever, not a weight-update technique.

- Paper: *Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback (InstructGPT)* — Ouyang et al., 2022 — the classical SFT → RM → PPO pipeline.
- Paper: *Direct Preference Optimization* — Rafailov et al., 2023 — DPO, the modern default for preference tuning.
- Paper: *Tülu 3: Pushing Frontiers in Open Language Model Post-Training* — AI2, 2024 — the canonical open SFT + DPO + RLVR recipe.
- Paper: *DeepSeekMath* — DeepSeek, 2024 — introduces GRPO, widely used with RLVR.
- Paper: *DeepSeek-R1* — DeepSeek, 2025 — large-scale RLVR for reasoning, including the R1-Zero "SFT-free" variant.
- Paper: *Solving Math Word Problems with Process- and Outcome-Based Feedback* — Uesato et al., 2022 — early verifiable-reward RL on math, predates the RLVR name.
- Paper: *KTO: Model Alignment as Prospect Theoretic Optimization* — Ethayarajh et al., 2024.
- Paper: *Let's Verify Step by Step* — Lightman et al., 2023 — process reward models.
